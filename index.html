Canonical public biography · Khalid Saqr

Khalid Saqr

A researcher-builder whose work moves from fluid dynamics and complex engineering systems to neurovascular modelling, Bio-CFD, mechanobiology, and the organisation of scientific knowledge.

Canonical URL: https://khalid-saqr.github.io/khalid-saqr/

A life in models, anomalies and usable knowledge

Saqr’s work has been a sustained study of systems that do not disclose their order at first sight. The setting has changed over time: buses and pipes, flames and turbines, aneurysms and arteries, papers and intellectual property. The underlying question has remained largely the same: what kind of instrument, model or structure is needed before a hidden signal can be seen clearly enough to be acted upon?

Engineering discipline

The early work began in the practical world of mechanical and thermal engineering. Air-conditioning control in buses, thermoelectric recovery from automobile exhaust, heat-transfer augmentation in pipes, thermoelectrics in power generation, swirling flow, confined vortices and Taylor–Couette instability were not glamorous problems. They were useful because they trained a discipline of attention. They required respect for established methods: conservation laws, numerical schemes, turbulence models, experimental comparison and the patient adjustment of models to physical reality.1–10

That patience became more important as the work entered vortices, combustion, detonation and turbulent flow. Spray atomisation, non-premixed flames, gas-turbine mixing, cylindrical cavities, soot and NOx formation, schlieren experiments, ideal detonations, pulse-detonation engines, whirling flames, vortex combustors, OpenFOAM implementation, variable-geometry swirlers, annular vortex decay, hydrogen addition and radiation modelling placed the same difficulty in different forms. A calculation could be made, but could it be trusted? A model could be run, but did it preserve the feature of the physical system that mattered?11–38

Computation as judgement

A central habit formed during this period: computation was not treated as a picture-making device, but as an instrument of judgement. In the work on urban pollutant dispersion, biomass flameless combustion, entropy generation, erosion-corrosion, natural convection, unconfined swirl, CFD parallelisation, turbulent pipe flow, surface roughness, greenhouse performance, Wells turbines, wind-turbine blades, exergy analysis, marine current turbines, subsea pipelines, passive flow control, lid-driven cavity benchmarks, trapped-vortex combustors, flameless combustors and phase-change materials, the common concern was not merely flow prediction. It was organised loss: the loss of energy, stability, efficiency, fidelity or design authority inside systems whose behaviour could not be understood by looking at their parts in isolation.39–61

The range of subjects was wide, but it was not scattered. Renewable-energy machines, environmental street canyons, marine structures, combustors and benchmark CFD problems were approached as complex systems in which geometry, walls, unsteadiness, turbulence, heat and material response shaped the outcome. This period gave the later biological work its intellectual machinery: suspicion of oversimplified assumptions, respect for physical scale and the habit of asking what the model fails to see.39–61,67,70

The turn to blood flow

The decisive turn came when that machinery was brought to the human circulation. Blood flow in large arteries and cerebral vessels did not behave as a passive engineering fluid moving through passive tubes. It carried pulses, vortices, near-wall fluctuations, viscosity effects, bifurcation dynamics, patient-specific geometry, compliant walls and biological consequence. The problem was no longer only how the flow moved. It was what the flow meant to living tissue.62–74

In studies of intracranial aneurysms, wall shear stress, non-Newtonian and Newtonian blood models, Doppler evidence, Moyamoya disease, carotid stenosis, carotid stenting, internal carotid bifurcations, compliant aneurysm walls, particle-image velocimetry, physiologic turbulence and energy cascade, earlier work on turbulence and complex flow found its biological subject. The artery became more than a domain for simulation. It became a place where mechanics and life meet.62,64–66,68–69,71–75,77–79

The vascular anomaly

The central anomaly was turbulence in human circulation, especially evidence that some vascular turbulence may not fit comfortably within the familiar Kolmogorov description. This was not a small technical correction. It reorganised the problem. If blood flow could contain spectral or turbulent structures that prevailing assumptions had not fully accounted for, then the vascular wall might be receiving mechanical information that conventional haemodynamic descriptors had not captured. A vortex, a pulse, a fluctuation, a shear pattern or a cascade might not merely be a flow feature. It might be part of the language by which blood speaks to tissue.71,74–75,77–79,81–82

That question led naturally into mechanobiology. The work on endothelial epigenetic response, mechano-miRNAs, aneurysm haemodynamics, wall compliance, carotid stenting, energy cascade, bone remodelling, Womersley flow, resonant spectral behaviour and picoNewton-scale force can be read as one widening inquiry: what does the vascular wall actually receive from the flow? The later studies do not abandon CFD. They make its purpose more exact. The flow field matters because it may carry a biological signal.66,74–79,81–82

Bio-CFD

Bio-CFD emerged from this crossing of engineering and medicine. It was not simply a change of application. It was the recognition that a model becomes important when it changes what clinicians, engineers and scientists can see, test and build. The move into neurovascular modelling was therefore not a departure from mechanical engineering. It was a more severe test of it. A boundary was no longer merely a wall. It was living tissue. A numerical field was no longer merely a solution. It was a possible explanation for disease.62,64–66,68–69,71–75,77–79,81–82

Some later and adjacent work may appear, at first glance, to fall outside this line: bone remodelling with fluid–structure interaction, hydropower exergy, wind-turbine performance in sandstorms and computational forensic linguistics. Yet these works reveal the same pattern. The recurring subject is the hidden structure of complex systems. Whether the system is a turbine, a flame, an urban canyon, a diseased vessel, a bone undergoing remodelling or a legal text carrying institutional authority, the task is to expose the structure that governs consequence.67,70,76,80

Knowledge as the next complex system

Over time, this way of thinking moved beyond fluid mechanics. Scientific knowledge itself began to appear as another complex system whose signals were badly organised. Papers, citations, code, data, simulations, expert judgement, intellectual property and institutional memory are spread across forms that humans struggle to audit and machines struggle to read. The problem was no longer only inside the artery or the combustor. It was inside the way knowledge is stored, owned, verified and reused.80–82

The present direction follows from that continuity. Saqr’s research has moved from modelling energy systems to modelling vortices, from vortices to turbulence, from turbulence to blood, from blood to mechanobiological signalling, and from there to the infrastructure of knowledge itself. The continuity is not a topic but a method: find the hidden signal inside a complex system; build the instrument that makes it visible; and make that visibility useful enough to change what people can test, trust and build.1–82
