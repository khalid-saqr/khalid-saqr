Khalid Saqr

Models, anomalies and trust.

Canonical URL: https://khalid-saqr.github.io/khalid-saqr/

Overview

By 2026, Khalid Saqr’s work had reached a precise question at the boundary of physics and life: what does a living vessel receive from blood flow? In studies of Womersley flow, picoNewton-scale force and resonant spectral cascade, the old language of fluid mechanics had become a way to ask how tissue may sense motion81,82. The question did not appear suddenly. It was the result of an intellectual journey through blood-flow anomalies, neurovascular modelling, combustion, entropy, heat transfer and control.

This page follows that journey in flashback. It begins with the mature question, then moves backward toward the earlier work that made the question possible. The same pattern recurs across the career: a system looks familiar, then a hidden condition makes it harder to understand, and a new instrument is needed before the result can be trusted.

The 2026 question

The late haemodynamic work is not only about simulating blood flow. It asks what physical signal reaches the wall of a living vessel. A transverse picoNewton force in anisotropic Womersley flow points to a scale at which pulsatile mechanics may matter for cellular sensing81. A resonant spectral cascade triggered by arterial geometry links vessel shape, pulsatile flow and spectral organisation as one mechanism82.

For a general reader, the point is simple: blood does not merely pass through a vessel. It pushes, fluctuates, rotates and changes near the wall. For a specialist, the question is sharper: which part of the flow field should be treated as biologically meaningful?

The vascular anomaly

The immediate path to the 2026 question ran through wall compliance, turbulence and the limits of ordinary assumptions. Patient-specific aneurysm experiments showed that wall elasticity can change instability and wall shear stress75, and later PIV experiments extended that question in a full-scale cerebral aneurysm model79. Carotid-stenosis work showed that stenting can alter near-wall turbulence, not merely vessel geometry77.

The central anomaly was physiological turbulence. A 2020 study argued that blood flow in the body can be turbulent71. The later non-Kolmogorov work asked whether vascular turbulence follows the familiar cascade assumed in classical turbulence, or whether blood flow carries a different mechanical signature with possible mechanobiological significance78. Energy-cascade analysis in an internal carotid artery bifurcation gave that question a vascular form by comparing pulsatile Newtonian and power-law models74.

The biological receiver also came into view. Endothelial studies connected different wall shear-stress magnitudes to epigenetic response and mechano-miRNAs66. Bone-remodelling work, although outside the artery, reinforced the wider idea that tissue responds to coupled mechanics, morphology and flow76.

When CFD became a problem about knowledge

From 2018 onward, blood-flow CFD no longer looked only like an engineering application. The deeper Khalid moved into intracranial haemodynamics, the more the problem became theoretical: what if the model class itself was not adequate for what living flow was doing?

Doppler evidence for non-Newtonian intracranial blood flow had already shown that cerebral blood could not always be treated as a simple engineering liquid62. Aneurysm-viscosity studies then tested how Newtonian, power-law and quasi-mechanistic models change the interpretation of vascular flow64,68,69. Moyamoya studies connected transient ischaemic attacks to haemodynamic complexity in diseased cerebral vessels63,72. A critical review of intracranial aneurysm CFD asked what the field had really learned from its simulations73.

The tension around these papers changed the direction of the career. The question was no longer only whether a simulation was accurate. It was how a scientific field decides that a model is adequate. That is where Thomas Kuhn became more than a name in the philosophy of science. The first version of the later KGX3 concern appeared as soph.io: an attempt to make claims, evidence and scientific transitions easier to represent and inspect.

Tohoku and the technology world

The Tohoku period placed neurovascular modelling in a setting where science, medicine and technology assessment could no longer be separated. Work on aneurysms, Moyamoya disease, physiological turbulence and wall response created subject-matter expertise relevant to endovascular repair and related technologies63,65,71,72,73,75,77,79.

That expertise later opened contact with the venture-capital world, where deep technical claims had to be judged before capital, product strategy or clinical translation could follow. Newchip Accelerator and ScienceWerx extended this path into a working expert network at the interface of deep technology, funding and private equity. This was not a departure from the research. It was the same question of trust moved into a different room: what evidence is strong enough to act on?

Return to Egypt

Khalid received his Ph.D. from Universiti Teknologi Malaysia in 2011, months after the Egyptian revolution. When he returned to Egypt in 2012, the high-speed combustion problems on which he had trained no longer seemed to be the only natural direction for his work. The transition toward biofluid applications began there, gradually and not always in a straight line.

The shift is best left partly open. It was not a single conversion from one topic to another. It was a change in what counted as a meaningful problem: from flow in machines to flow in living systems, from combustion chambers to vessels, and from prediction to interpretation.

The combustion apprenticeship

Looking further back, the combustion work explains why turbulence later became such a natural language for vascular flow. Spray-atomisation studies followed the breakup of liquid into droplets, where a continuous stream becomes a dispersed population whose behaviour must be reconstructed mathematically11,20. Non-premixed flame studies asked how surrounding turbulence changes combustion12, and soot and NOx work showed that flame structure also changes what combustion leaves behind15.

Explosion and detonation studies trained a practical respect for the relation between observation and calculation. Schlieren imaging made confined explosions and combustion kernels visible as optical events16,17; Boltzmann-equation notes and ideal-detonation calculations treated the same fast processes as mathematical structure18,23. Pulse-detonation work connected transient mixing, detonation and measurable thrust22,24,32.

Vortex-flame and combustor studies made rotation a central theme. Shear-driven cavities, whirling flames, asymmetric vortex flames and variable-geometry swirlers showed that a vortex is not only a pattern but a mechanism for organising mixing and instability14,19,25,27,29,35. OpenFOAM implementation, eddy-dissipation modelling, probability-density-function comparison and radiation-model integration then tested which assumptions survive when combustion theory is placed inside working software28,30,36. Curved-duct, unconfined-swirl and annular-vortex studies extended the same caution to turbulence closures under strained, swirling and decaying conditions21,31,45.

Hydrogen-enriched flames and entropy generation turned combustion into a study of consequence. Hydrogen addition changed flame structure and emissions34, while entropy generation in swirl-stabilised combustion measured where useful energy was degraded inside the flow37,38. The highway energy study widened the same logic from a flame or engine to infrastructure, treating energy demand as a system to be anticipated26. Khalid later published his Ph.D. work as Turbulent Vortex Flames.

Loss, design and diagnosis

The middle work, seen in flashback, shows how engineering modelling became a way to diagnose where systems lose performance. Urban street-canyon studies made pollution a question of geometry, wind and public space33,39. Biomass flameless combustion asked how fuel, temperature and reaction structure could be arranged for cleaner conversion40. Natural-convection and turbulent-pipe studies used entropy to locate where useful energy disappears41,44,47.

Renewable-energy papers developed the same habit in machines exposed to real environments. Wells-turbine studies linked wave-energy conversion to viscous loss and entropy generation42,50,56,58. Wind-turbine work treated a blade as a surface whose performance changes under roughness, dust and sandstorm conditions48,51,70. Marine-current turbine and subsea-pipeline studies moved the problem into water, angle of attack, protection methods and offshore loading53,55.

Other applied studies broadened the same pattern. Erosion-corrosion in seawater fittings showed damage emerging from flow and particles together43. Solar-greenhouse work joined agriculture, thermal regulation and photovoltaic performance in one operating system49. Combined-cycle and hydropower exergy studies moved the diagnostic scale from local flow to whole-plant efficiency52,67. CFD parallelisation and lid-driven cavity benchmarking asked whether the computational instruments themselves were adequate for the physics being investigated46,57.

By this point, combustor and building studies were also being framed through criteria rather than appearance. RANS assessment of trapped-vortex combustors and CFD criteria for flameless combustion treated recirculation and mixing as design evidence59,60. Phase-change-material work treated thermal comfort as the outcome of a coupled system61. Partially open cavity studies retained the same concern with geometry, turbulence and heat transfer under changing boundary conditions54.

The early discipline of heat and control

At the beginning of the flashback are practical engineering problems. Bus air-conditioning studies asked how a cooling system should decide under changing heat and humidity, turning comfort into a control problem1,2. Thermoelectric work asked how waste heat from an exhaust pipe could become useful power rather than discarded energy3,7. Pipe heat-transfer work made the same question local and geometric, showing how internal fins alter flow and heat exchange9.

The early CFD papers moved from energy systems toward the hidden organisation of flow. A Zanker plate and a gas-turbine swirler were treated as devices that create turbulence, mixing and structure inside a moving fluid4,5,13. Confined-vortex and Taylor–Couette studies placed rotation and instability at the centre of the problem8,10. The question of whether large-eddy simulation could predict transition made explicit what would stay with the later work: computation was being tested as a way of seeing what ordinary observation could not resolve6.

Return to the present

The flashback ends where the page began: with models, anomalies and trust. Bio-CFD grew from the attempt to make vascular simulation useful to medicine. KNOWDYN carried the same concern into knowledge capital: papers, code, datasets, claims, simulations, expert judgement and intellectual property treated as materials that must be structured before they can be valued or reused.

The Thomas Kuhn Foundation gives a philosophical and institutional form to the anomaly-driven pattern in the research. The computational forensic analysis of judicial language showed that hidden structure can also govern authority outside physics80. KGX3 continues the same concern as a technical layer for representing claims, evidence and scientific transitions in machine-readable form. ScienceWerx extends the concern outward, where scientific knowledge must become institutional, economic and regional capability.

Across these later institutions, the point is not to leave science for business. It is to carry the same discipline of modelling into the systems that decide what science can become: evidence, ownership, judgement, investment and trust.
