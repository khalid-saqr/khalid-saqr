Autobiography

Every achievement begins with a question that refuses to go away. Mine began in fluid dynamics

I was trained to respect established models, use status-quo instruments, and I have developed the patience for modelling complex systems over two decades. Yet, in the human circulation, I found a difficult kind of problem: blood flow that did not sit comfortably inside the usual assumptions. It was not merely a calculation to be improved. It was an anomaly, and anomalies have always been the seed of new science.

Following that observation carried me from computational fluid dynamics into neurovascular modelling and Bio-CFD. The work was not abstract. It asked how a force, a vortex, a wall, or a pulse of blood might matter inside a living vessel. It connected engineering to medicine, and it made clear that a model is valuable only when it changes what people can see, test, and build.

Over time the problem widened. Scientific knowledge itself was behaving like a poorly connected system. Papers, code, data, citations, intellectual property, and expert judgement were scattered across places that machines could not easily read and people could not easily trust. I now work on that missing layer: systems that make evidence, simulation, and invention structured enough to be inspected, reused, and defended.

Current direction

The next scientific revolution will not come only from publishing more papers. It will come from making evidence, simulations, claims, code, data, and expert judgement easier to connect, verify, and reuse. My current work is focused on building that missing layer: scientific knowledge that machines can inspect and humans can act on.
